Fast Fashion Impact
Visualization Documentation

Project Documentation

Interactive visualization of the environmental, labor, and social costs of fast fashion, built with D3.js.

Dataset

The dataset used is The True Cost of Fast Fashion Impact by Khushi Yadav on Kaggle (MIT license). It contains 3,000 records across 25 attributes covering brand identity, production volume, environmental impact (carbon, water, landfill), labor conditions (wages, working hours, child-labor incidents), social-media attention, sentiment, and sustainability / ethics scores.

Design Process

When I first sat down with the Fast Fashion dataset, the sheer size and scope of it made the design challenge immediately clear. With dozens of attributes spanning environmental impact, labor conditions, and financial data, trying to visualize everything at once would produce something overwhelming and unreadable. So my first step was simply to read through the data carefully and ask: what story does this actually want to tell?

That process of exploration helped me narrow the focus down to three distinct angles I could build a visualization around: environmental cost (CO₂ emissions, water consumption, and waste per garment), human cost (worker wages, labor conditions, and hours worked), and financial cost (retail pricing versus true production cost). Each angle was compelling on its own, but I had to pick one to start. I chose environmental cost, both because the data felt richest there and because it connected to a question that felt genuinely urgent: which brands and garment types are doing the most damage to the planet, and by how much?

First Attempts

My initial instinct was to use a brushable scatterplot — a chart type I found in the D3 gallery that plots two variables on X and Y axes while encoding a third variable through dot size. The idea was to map CO₂ on one axis, water consumption on the other, and use dot size to represent waste. In theory, this would let a viewer see all three environmental dimensions simultaneously and brush over clusters of points to filter the view.

In practice, it was harder to read than I expected. The dots overlapped significantly, the three-variable encoding felt cognitively heavy, and it was not immediately obvious what the viewer was supposed to take away from it. The visualization technically worked, but it did not communicate.

Finding a Better Model

At that point I stepped back and looked for inspiration in how other data-driven sites handled similar complexity. The site that stuck with me most was Realtime Inequality, which does something clever: rather than forcing the viewer to hold multiple questions in their head at once, it gives them one clear view at a time while making it easy to switch between different lenses on the same underlying data. The interface feels guided without being restrictive.

That reframing changed how I thought about my own project. Instead of picking just one of the three cost angles and losing the others entirely, I could show all three — environmental, human, and financial — but in a way that kept each view clean and focused. The viewer is not overwhelmed because they are only ever looking at one dimension at a time, but the full picture is always within reach.

Refining the Design

Once I decided to show all three dimensions, I made several deliberate choices to improve readability across the board. For the environmental cost view, I replaced the scatterplot with a grouped bar chart. Bar charts are one of the most intuitive visual formats available: viewers can instantly compare heights across categories without needing to decode overlapping dots or interpret bubble sizes. For comparing CO₂, water, and waste across different brands, a bar chart made the differences legible at a glance in a way the scatterplot never quite achieved.

For the human cost and financial cost views, I applied the same single-year default and expandable range approach, but returned to the scatterplot format, this time with a key improvement. Rather than relying solely on color to distinguish brands, I encoded brand identity through distinct shapes. Color alone can be difficult to track when multiple brands cluster together in the same region of a chart, but pairing color with shape gives the viewer two simultaneous visual cues to latch onto. A viewer who spots a triangle always knows which brand they are looking at, even when the dots are dense. This made it noticeably easier to follow individual brands across the chart without constantly cross-referencing the legend.

Across all three views, I also added a key metrics panel that displays contextual averages alongside the charts. One limitation of raw comparison charts is that they tell you which brand is worse than another but not whether any of them are actually bad in absolute terms. The key metrics section addresses that by grounding the visual comparisons in real-world averages, giving the viewer a meaningful benchmark and making the severity of the worst offenders much harder to ignore.

For the overall layout, I defaulted every view to a single year rather than displaying the full time range at once. Showing all years simultaneously added visual noise without adding much insight. Anchoring to one year gives the viewer a clear baseline, and they can expand the range themselves if they want to explore trends over time.

Rationale of Design Choices

Every visual decision in this project was made with one goal in mind: reduce the cognitive effort required to extract a meaningful insight.

Chart Type Per Dimension

The environmental cost view uses a grouped bar chart because the core question it answers is a comparison question: how do brands rank against each other on CO₂, water, and waste? Bar charts are the single most effective chart type for direct magnitude comparison. The human and financial cost views use scatterplots because those questions are relational, and scatterplots are built for revealing relationships between two continuous variables simultaneously.

Color Encoding

Color is used exclusively to encode brand identity, not to encode a data value. This was a deliberate choice. When color is used to encode a quantitative variable (like a heatmap), it forces the viewer to mentally map a hue back to a number, which adds friction. Since brand identity is categorical and already labeled in a legend, color here serves as a fast recognition shortcut: once a viewer associates a color with a particular brand, they can track that brand across the chart instantly without reading any text.

Shape Encoding

On the scatterplots, brand identity is encoded through both color and shape. This redundant encoding is intentional. Color alone fails when points overlap or when a viewer has color vision deficiency. Shape provides a second, independent visual channel that carries the same information. A circle, triangle, and square remain distinguishable even when rendered small, clustered, or printed in grayscale. Using both together means no viewer is left without a reliable way to tell brands apart.

Single-Year Default With Expand Option

Defaulting to one year reduces the number of data points visible at first load, which lowers visual clutter and lets the viewer orient themselves before adding complexity. The ability to expand to multiple years is preserved so that viewers who want to explore trends over time are not blocked from doing so, but that complexity is opt-in rather than imposed.

Key Metrics Panel Placement

The key metrics panel is placed adjacent to each chart rather than at the top of the page. This keeps the contextual average close to the visual it relates to, so the viewer does not have to remember a number they saw elsewhere on the page. Proximity reinforces the connection between the benchmark and the chart it anchors.

Tab-Based Navigation Between Dimensions

Arranging the three cost dimensions as tabs rather than stacking all three charts vertically was a layout decision rooted in focus. Stacking all charts would give the viewer everything at once, but it would also create a long scroll where the environmental chart might be forgotten by the time the viewer reaches the financial one. Tabs enforce one view at a time, which mirrors how a viewer would naturally think through one question before moving to the next.

Findings

Finding 1: Production Volume and Carbon Emissions

Setting X = Production (tonnes/mo) and Y = Carbon Emissions (tCO₂e) reveals a roughly positive relationship, but with substantial scatter — some brands achieve high output with comparatively lower emissions, while others show disproportionately high emissions at moderate production levels.

Finding 2: Sustainability vs. Environmental Cost

Plotting Sustainability Score against Env Cost Index shows an expected negative trend: higher sustainability scores correlate with lower environmental cost indices. However, the relationship is noisy, suggesting that sustainability scores alone do not fully capture real environmental impact.

Finding 3: Worker Wages Across Countries

Using the Country color encoding with Avg Worker Wage on the Y-axis highlights geographic pay disparities: records from certain countries cluster at markedly lower wage levels. Brushing these clusters confirms that low-wage records also tend to have higher working hours per week.
